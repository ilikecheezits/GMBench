Good Model Project

Good Model Project is a proposed nonprofit built on a simple idea: organizations serving their communities should not be the last to benefit from AI. We help under-resourced nonprofits identify practical use cases, set up the right tools, and build workflows that make their teams more capable with the resources they already have.

# Background and Vision

!image.png

## Introduction

AI adoption is increasingly constrained not by access to models, but by access to implementation capacity.

Most nonprofits now have some path to powerful AI tools, but access is not the same as use. Under-resourced organizations typically lack the staff, budget, and operational slack required to evaluate where AI fits, build workflows around it, and maintain those workflows over time. The organizations that could benefit most from AI-supported workflows are often the least equipped to adopt them well.

Good Model Project is a proposed nonprofit focused on closing that gap. If AI can raise institutional capacity, that capacity should not be the exclusive privilege of well-funded organizations.

The long-term ambition is to build a public-interest AI deployment and infrastructure layer for nonprofits and under-resourced institutions. GMP would begin with real operational needs in the field, support careful deployments, and turn repeated patterns into reusable infrastructure. Some of that infrastructure may remain free, some open-source, some offered at cost. If value is created, it should flow back into the ecosystem as cheaper deployments and stronger nonprofit support.

The deeper concern is equity. AI is becoming infrastructure for institutional capacity, and the communities that will most depend on it should have a hand in shaping it — not only in receiving it.

---

## The Challenge Ahead

Nonprofits are under-resourced in three related ways.

#### *Implementation*

AI systems are still difficult to deploy well. Most nonprofits do not have staff who can evaluate where AI fits, configure tools responsibly, build usable workflows, and maintain the result. Even when staff are interested, they may not know where to begin or how to tell whether a system is actually helping.

The hard part is rarely model access alone. The hard part is understanding a real workflow and translating it into something useful. This is why forward-deployed engineers and implementation specialists have become so valuable in the private sector. Good Model Project is an attempt to bring some version of that capacity to organizations that would otherwise not have access to it.

#### *Adoption cost and access*

Many nonprofits cannot support technical employees, consultants, SaaS subscriptions, API usage, or sustained experimentation. A donated license helps, but it does not map workflows, train staff, establish governance, or maintain a system after setup. At the same time, even modest software or usage costs can become a barrier for smaller organizations.

Good Model Project should eventually help solve both sides of this problem: implementation capacity and affordable access. That may mean donated credits, free licenses, subsidized software, open-source tools, or at-cost deployments. The goal is to help nonprofits adopt useful systems without adding new financial burden.

#### *Continuity*

Nonprofits often operate with high staff turnover, volunteer turnover, and fragmented documentation. If one staff member learns a system and then leaves, the organization may lose the ability to maintain it. If a volunteer builds something brittle and disappears, the nonprofit inherits the maintenance burden.

Durable capacity requires maintainable systems, not just trained individuals. It requires documentation, shared workflows, clear ownership, and support structures that can survive turnover.

---

## Non-Goals

Good Model Project should be disciplined about what it is trying to do: capacity building over evangelism and fundraising.

The purpose is not to persuade every nonprofit to adopt AI. Some organizations may not need it. Some may not want it. Some operate in areas where the risks outweigh the benefits. The work is to identify where AI can responsibly improve operational capacity — helping a food pantry process intake more efficiently, or helping staff find answers across scattered documents without relying only on institutional memory.

The emphasis is also on capacity building rather than fundraising alone. Grants and donations matter; they help organizations survive. But many nonprofits also face recurring administrative burdens that persist regardless of short-term funding cycles. Improving intake workflows, reporting systems, internal coordination, or communication infrastructure may create more durable operational gains. The focus belongs on systems and processes that make organizations more effective over time.

Furthermore, GMP should not replace frontline human care, automate sensitive judgment without oversight, or chase novel technology for its own sake. It should not build tools nonprofits cannot maintain. It should not optimize for volunteer experience at the expense of nonprofit impact. It should not treat every operational problem as an AI problem.

The purpose is not to maximize automation. It is to responsibly increase capacity.

---

## The End Game

If Good Model Project works, it becomes more than a volunteer implementation network. It becomes a public-interest AI infrastructure layer for organizations that would otherwise be left behind.

Nonprofits would gain not only tools but implementation capacity, reusable workflows, maintained systems, and ongoing support. Good Model Labs could eventually build software for entire categories of community institutions — pantries, shelters, clinics, schools, legal aid, animal rescues, and other local service providers. Some of that software may remain free, some open-source, some offered at cost.

AI will create institutional advantages. The question is who gets to use them, who shapes them, and who benefits. If deployment is left only to commercial incentives, the best-supported institutions will continue to move faster while under-resourced organizations fall further behind. The gap will not be only technological. It will be operational.

Good Model Project exists to narrow that gap — to help communities build and share in the infrastructure that makes AI useful.

# Operating Model

!image.png

## Deployment Strategy

GMP would operate across four levels of support — a way to clarify what kind of help a nonprofit actually needs, not a rigid menu of services.

**1. Needs assessment.** Many organizations do not initially need a custom AI system. They need help understanding where operational friction exists and whether AI is an appropriate intervention at all. This means interviewing staff, mapping workflows, and identifying where time is being lost. Sometimes the right answer does not involve AI.

**2. AI setup.** Helping organizations use existing tools safely and effectively — selecting the right products, configuring them well, and training staff to rely on them appropriately. Many nonprofits will never need a custom build. A clean, well-governed setup using existing tools may produce meaningful improvement on its own.

**3. Workflow automation.** Turning repeated work into durable workflows — intake summarization, multilingual communication, recurring reporting, and similar operational patterns. The test is whether the workflow makes the organization meaningfully easier to operate. A technically impressive workflow that saves no time, creates maintenance burden, or increases risk should not be considered successful.

**4. Custom build (Good Model Labs).** For cases where existing tools are not enough. Labs would build reusable tools from problems that appear repeatedly across deployments. Labs should not chase novelty. Its best work comes from repetition — seeing the same problem across several organizations, understanding it well, and building something that reduces the cost of solving it again.

## Focus areas

GMP could eventually organize around major domains:

- Good Model for Health
- Good Model for Education
- Good Model for Climate
- Good model for Animal Welfare

… and others. The purpose would be to develop domain expertise, shared project patterns, governance standards, and mentor networks suited to each setting.

A health project should have different standards than a food pantry project. A legal aid project should have different safeguards than an animal shelter project. Domain focus allows the organization to become sharper over time rather than spread evenly thin.

---

## Examples Deployments

#### ***Food pantry***

A pantry may need help with intake, volunteer coordination, and recurring reporting. GMP would first map where staff time is being spent, then help set up a shared workspace and build workflows around the highest-friction tasks. If the same needs appear across several pantries, Labs could turn those patterns into reusable infrastructure that other pantries adopt with minimal setup. The goal is to learn from one pantry deeply enough to support many pantries better.

#### ***Community helplines and shelters***

Many community organizations — domestic violence shelters, legal aid groups, and immigrant support organizations among them — rely heavily on phone calls, voicemail, and follow-up. These are sensitive environments, so the governance standard would need to be high. With the right safeguards, Labs could focus on a narrow and recurring problem: missed-call triage. A useful system would transcribe voicemails, separate administrative calls from urgent ones, and help staff prioritize callbacks. It would not replace staff judgment or autonomously handle emergencies. It would help overloaded teams understand what came in and what requires attention.

#### ***Climate organizations***

A climate-focused team might work with, say, restoration nonprofits for kelp farms. These organizations often face repeated administrative work around grant reporting and compliance documentation. Rather than proposing dozens of disconnected AI ideas, GMP could spend months understanding one operational problem deeply, then build shared templates that reduce administrative work across similar organizations. The value comes from depth and reuse rather than breadth.

## Organizational Structure

**Operations.** Running deployments and measuring whether they actually help.

- *Deployments:* nonprofit intake, project scoping, and ongoing support.
- *Impact:* measurement, case studies, and ethical review. Asks whether projects are actually improving capacity, preventing the organization from confusing activity with usefulness.
- *Education:* volunteer training, nonprofit training, playbooks, and reusable materials.

**Labs.** Turning deployment patterns into shared infrastructure.

- *Applied:* lightweight tools for recurring problems, such as food pantry operations or missed-call triage.
- *Research:* anonymized needs assessments and studies of AI-enabled nonprofit capacity.

**Development.** Bringing partnerships, funding, and people into the organization.

- *Partnerships:* sponsors, philanthropic relationships, AI companies, and university programs.
- *Recruitment:* sourcing volunteers, mentors, and nonprofit partners. Maintains a high bar rather than optimizing for volume.

## Operating principles

1. **The nonprofit is the beneficiary.** Volunteers will learn, students will gain experience, and companies may identify talent along the way. These are secondary effects. If a project is not useful to the nonprofit, it should not continue simply because it is interesting to contributors.
2. **Capacity matters more than activity.** The organization should not measure itself by workshops run, tools introduced, or prompts written. The question is whether the nonprofit became more capable — whether staff saved time, continuity improved, fewer tasks fell through the cracks, or the workflow remained usable after the original team left.
3. **Small teams over large clubs.** The ideal unit is a small team supported by an experienced mentor, rather than a large chapter. This avoids the common failure mode where many people join, few contribute meaningfully, and the beneficiary becomes secondary.
4. **Partnerships should be durable.** Long-term partnerships with one nonprofit over multiple years create context, trust, and continuity. Teams gain a clearer understanding of what has already been tried, what constraints exist, and what is genuinely useful.

Good Model Project requires two forms of discipline.

- *Implementation* is the work of understanding a process, configuring tools, and making the result usable. It resembles the work of a forward-deployed engineer or implementation specialist. Can the team turn a messy operational workflow into a functioning system?
- *Impact* is the work of defining the actual problem, determining whether a workflow is producing meaningful improvement, and making sure the implementation is useful in practice. It resembles the work of a product lead, operator, or evaluator. Did the system actually improve the organization's capacity?

Both questions matter. A workflow can function correctly and still be useless. It can produce polished outputs and save no time. It can automate a task that should not have been automated, create false confidence, or introduce new maintenance burden.

#### On why a national network

A national network creates value that isolated local chapters cannot easily create on their own.

1. *Shared infrastructure.* Many nonprofits face similar operational problems. If one team develops something useful — an intake workflow, a training module, an integration pattern — other teams should be able to reuse it.
2. *Governance.* AI deployment requires standards. A central organization can define how nonprofits are vetted, how sensitive information is handled, and what kinds of projects are appropriate. This becomes especially important if the organization eventually controls donated credits, licenses, or shared infrastructure.
3. *Mentorship.* A healthcare project benefits from someone with healthcare technology experience; a legal aid project from someone in legal tech. These mentors do not need to be local.
4. *Funding.* A national organization with measurable deployments, governance standards, case studies, and reusable infrastructure can make a stronger case to donors, foundations, and technology partners than a single local group.
5. *Compounding learning.* Each deployment improves the organization's understanding of nonprofit operations and responsible implementation. Over time, that knowledge becomes an institutional asset.

## Funding and Partnerships

GMP should not rely entirely on traditional philanthropy if a more durable model is possible. The long-term structure may look closer to a public-interest social enterprise: free for organizations that cannot pay, subsidized where possible, at-cost where appropriate, and open-source when useful.

Technology providers may become important partners through donated credits, discounted licenses, infrastructure support, or technical guidance. This work should be welcomed. But donated access alone is not enough. A license does not map workflows. Free credits do not train staff. Tool access does not create governance or maintenance capacity. Good Model Project would operate at the adoption layer, and if it can demonstrate a repeatable adoption model, major AI companies and software providers could become partners rather than substitutes.

Several funding sources could work together: donated AI credits and software licenses; individual and recurring donations tied to concrete outcomes ("fund a workflow," "cover one nonprofit for six months"); sponsored domain work in areas such as food access, legal aid, or climate; at-cost deployments for larger nonprofits that can pay; open-source Labs tools that reduce the marginal cost of future deployments; and research and workforce partnerships, with appropriate consent and anonymization.

Funding should reinforce capacity building rather than push the organization toward shallow deployment metrics.

## A Retrospective (Student-Service Models)

There is a long history of student-service models — college students supporting local organizations through volunteering, consulting, or community education. Some have produced real value. But they share recurring weaknesses: students are often inexperienced, may not understand why a problem exists, and may not know what kind of intervention would actually help. A consulting group may produce a strategy deck that is not actionable. A volunteer technology project may create maintenance burden. Well-intentioned teams may solve the wrong problem.

AI changes the opportunity but does not remove the risk. Small, well-supported teams can now do more useful operational work than before — organizing internal knowledge, improving intake, translating materials, summarizing meetings, or reducing administrative overhead. But if the team does not understand the workflow, the implementation will still fail.

Good Model Project cannot be structured primarily as a student club. Students — especially college students and exceptional high schoolers who can participate over multiple years — may become an important scaling mechanism. But the project should be organized around contribution quality rather than membership volume. Small, accountable, implementation-oriented teams supported by experienced mentors will go further than a large chapter with shallow involvement.

# The Path Forward

!image.png

## Risks

*Poor fit or lack of demand.* Some nonprofits will not want AI help. Demand will vary by sector, and the project will need to distinguish between inappropriate AI use and appropriate operational support — between generating superficial marketing content and reducing real administrative burden.

*Misaligned student incentives.* A project like this could attract people who want affiliation but not responsibility. The operating unit should be contribution, not membership.

*Inexperience causing harm.* Volunteers may lack the judgment needed for sensitive operational work. Without supervision, they could build brittle systems, mishandle data, misunderstand nonprofit needs, or automate the wrong process. This requires careful scoping, mentor oversight, training, access control, and clear boundaries on what volunteers can implement.

*Cybersecurity and privacy.* Nonprofits often handle sensitive information about vulnerable populations. GMP would need a serious security posture from the beginning — conservative about data, permissions, vendors, storage, and model use. Some projects should not be attempted until appropriate governance exists.

*Vetting and politics.* A centralized network raises hard questions about which nonprofits should be supported. GMP needs a broad moral compass and practical governance — willing to serve a wide range of organizations while retaining the ability to decline work that creates legal, ethical, reputational, or safety risk.

*Fraud and abuse.* If the project controls free licenses, credits, tools, or infrastructure, it will need to prevent misuse. Access should be gated by clear standards, not treated as a generic free resource.

*Superficial deployment volume.* The project could become less useful if funders or internal incentives reward shallow metrics. The goal is capacity, not activity.

---

## Open Questions

*Legal and governance.* Should the project begin before receiving 501(c)(3) status, or should the legal structure come first? How much early work can be funded personally before formal accounting becomes necessary? What governance is required before handling sensitive data, and what kinds of projects should be categorically excluded?

*Membership.* What should membership mean? How does someone contribute with only ten hours available? What is the minimum bar, and how does the organization prevent itself from becoming a loose student club?

*Access and abuse.* How should donated licenses or credits be allocated? Who qualifies, and what happens if a nonprofit misuses the tools?

*Nonprofit selection.* How are partners vetted? Should the project begin only with local organizations, or eventually work nationally and internationally? How should politically sensitive organizations be evaluated?

*Impact measurement.* What evidence is enough to claim that a deployment worked? How should quantitative measures such as time saved or response speed be weighed against qualitative signals such as continuity, staff confidence, and reduced operational stress?

*Lessons from earlier models.* What can be learned from earlier student-service organizations — which produced real capacity, which became too student-centered, and which failed to scale quality alongside size?

---

## Launch Path

**Alpha (1-2 months).** The first phase should be narrow and personally led by the initial team. Create a brief website. Identify a local nonprofit (per teammate) with enough operational volume to make the work meaningful. Scope and implement the first project. Measure whether it improves capacity and develop the first case study. Document the process and lessons. 

**Beta (1-2 months).** The second phase introduces a larger team. Recruit 1-3 additional people to the deployment. Target similar organizations from the alpha to validate a repeatable scoping process and basic volunteer training. Establish security and data-access rules. Document reusable workflows. 

**Launch.** Only after alpha and beta should GMP consider a broader network — local and remote teams, domain-specific focus areas, national mentors, shared playbooks, donated or subsidized AI access, Good Model Labs, research partnerships, impact measurement, and serious governance. The network should scale only if quality can scale with it.
